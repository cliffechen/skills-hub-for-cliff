# Humanizer Principles for Amazon Supplement Image Copy

Adapted from the local `humanizer-main` skill reviewed in this project. Source license: MIT License, Copyright (c) 2025 Siqi Chen. This file does not copy the full skill; it translates the relevant editing rules into a product-image copy workflow.

## Goal

Make supplement image copy sound like it came from a sharp brand copywriter, not from a generic AI marketing prompt. Keep compliance intact, but do not let compliance turn the copy into a label facts dump.

## Run this pass for

- A+ hero modules
- slogans
- black-panel manifesto copy
- paid-social-style image text
- brand philosophy sections
- any output the user says has "AI taste"

## Product copy AI tells to remove

1. Fake depth
   - Avoid lines that sound profound but mean little.
   - Watch for: "the language of," "the future of," "from the inside out," "where performance begins," "the foundation of vitality."
   - Replace with a sharper product thought or a concrete buyer feeling.

2. Symmetrical slogan formulas
   - Avoid overly balanced slogans that feel generated.
   - Watch for: "One active. One daily ritual.", "Clear formula. Clear label.", "Less noise. More intention."
   - Keep only if the layout needs extreme minimalism. Otherwise make it more specific.

3. Rule of three
   - Do not force three benefits.
   - If there are two strong ideas, use two. If there is one, let one stand.

4. "Not just X, but Y"
   - Avoid contrast formulas.
   - Say the point directly.

5. Stacked abstract nouns
   - Avoid: vitality, optimization, performance, intention, foundation, potential, transformation, journey, wellness, clarity.
   - Use concrete body/routine language where possible: daily, steady, capsule, serving, cell, energy metabolism, label.

6. Promotional filler
   - Avoid: revolutionary, breakthrough, ultimate, powerful, advanced, premium, cutting-edge, science-backed, expertly formulated unless proof is supplied.
   - If the claim needs proof, either cite the proof or remove the phrase.

7. Perfectly even cadence
   - Vary the rhythm. Pair one short line with one longer, grounded sentence.
   - A good hero can feel slightly unfinished or restrained. Do not explain everything.

8. Fake-candid openers
   - Avoid: "Here's the thing," "Let's be honest," "The truth is."
   - Start with the product idea.

9. Generic positive endings
   - Avoid "built for your best self," "made for modern life," "designed for your wellness journey."
   - End with a specific routine, serving, or product truth.

10. Over-clean compliance copy
   - Compliance should shape the copy, not become the copy.
   - Do not lead a hero with "clearly dosed," "label-led," or "facts shown as listed" unless the creative direction is intentionally clinical.

11. Repeating the same label facts across images
   - Avoid making every A+ image repeat the same dose, serving size, serving count, or support-claim triad.
   - Repetition makes the set feel like a facts dump instead of a product story.
   - Give each image a distinct job: story, routine, mechanism, formula facts, proof, comparison, or brand philosophy.
   - If an image has a narrative zone and a feature zone, use the narrative zone to explain why the ingredient matters; keep the feature zone for concise facts.

12. Wheel-spinning with new words
   - Do not treat synonym swaps as a new angle. "Start with the cell," "support below the surface," and "cell-first wellness" can all be the same idea.
   - A new image needs a new buyer question, new proof point, new objection answered, or new brand/packaging idea.
   - If the next image has no fresh evidence or brand material, ask the user for direction instead of producing polished repetition.
   - For brand philosophy modules, avoid dragging the copy back to dose, serving size, Supplement Facts, or the support triad. Write about design intent, product standards, packaging choices, brand restraint, or the buyer experience if those are supported by the visual.

## Better patterns for this supplement category

Use concrete tension:

- "Your cells do not need motivation. They need support."
- "Aging is not a mood. It is biology."
- "The routine is small. The system it supports is not."
- "Two softgels. One active. No crowded blend."
- "For the part of energy you do not feel until it fades."
- "Aging doesn't start in the mirror."
- "Start with the cell."
- "Not another crowded blend."
- "Nothing buried in a blend."
- "Read the label. Then decide."

Use controlled imperfection:

- Let one line be plain.
- Let one word carry the idea.
- Do not over-explain a slogan in the same visual zone.

Use specific product anchors:

- Urolithin A
- 1,000 mg per serving
- 2 capsules or softgels, after final label check
- 45 servings
- cellular health support, mitochondrial function support, and energy metabolism support only when substantiated

## Humanizer review loop

Before final output:

1. Read the copy aloud.
2. Mark lines that could fit any supplement.
3. Mark abstract words that do not point to a product fact.
4. Remove forced symmetry.
5. Mark facts or claims repeated from earlier images.
6. Replace one generic slogan with one sharper line.
7. Mark repeated meanings even when the wording is different.
8. Replace repeated facts with a new supported angle when the layout allows it.
9. If no new supported angle exists, ask the user rather than writing filler.
10. Keep only the claim level that compliance can support.

## Deliverable addition

For hero or slogan work, include:

- "AI taste removed": list the stale phrases you avoided.
- "Recommended version": one final version, not only a menu of options.
- "Bolder option": one higher-energy option with risk notes.

## Locked V3 taste benchmark

Use this as the preferred taste benchmark for The Ordinary-inspired supplement hero modules. Do not repeat it blindly for every product; match its human rhythm and restraint.

Recommended hero structure:

```text
Aging doesn't start in the mirror.

Start with the cell.

Not another crowded blend.

One active: Urolithin A.
1,000 mg per serving.

Made to support cellular health, mitochondrial function,
and energy metabolism.*

2 capsules. 45 servings.
```

Why this works:

- The first line has tension. It does not sound like a generic supplement slogan.
- The second line is short enough for a hero layout.
- The black-panel headline has attitude and product positioning.
- The factual lines stay close to Supplement Facts.
- The support claims are marked as structure/function claims that need substantiation and review.

Avoid drifting back to:

- "Age well, from the cell up."
- "Daily support for cellular energy."
- "The cellular routine for how you show up."
- "One active. One daily ritual."
- "Clear formula. Clear label."
- "Less noise. More intention."
